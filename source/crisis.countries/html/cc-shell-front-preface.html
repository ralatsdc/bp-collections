PREFACE

"Tangible fragments of life" -- "oddball collections of trinkets amidst compendia of data" -- "open-ended juxtapositions". All descriptions of the assemblage artwork by the American artist Joseph Cornell (1903-1972).

In May 2014, Joseph Cornell's assemblage "Untitled (Penny Arcade Portrait of Lauren Bacall)" sold at a Christie's auction for $5.3 million, setting an auction record for a work by this self-taught artist.

Rarely leaving New York City, Joseph Cornell lived in a series of small houses in Queens, and devoted himself to caring for his mother and disabled brother. He assembled everyday found objects -- marbles, paper, photos, wood, and glass -- in shadow boxes creating evocative imaginary worlds. His work is difficult to place within an art ovement or style, and has inspired generations of American artists.

Unlike Joseph Cornell who worked with physical objects, Blu-Pen works with virtual objects - content drawn from the internet, that vast compendium of everyday human expression.

While Joseph Cornell individually selected and juxtaposed tangible objects to express his personal statement, Blu Pen assembles crowd selected content. Stewart Culin, the late "museum magician" of the Brooklyn Museum, captured Blu Pen's essense well, we are "preserving the seed of things which may blossom and fruit again".

This collection, Blu Pen's first, orginates from social media content from countries experiencing humanitarian crisis. The content forms a narrative not shaped by a curator, but forged by the internet itself. Insignts and meaning are gained by the viewer as they navigate and explore the collection, a way that performs in the middle ground between print books, ebooks and websites.

We hope you'll enjoy exploring and ultimately be inspired by this Blu Pen collection.
