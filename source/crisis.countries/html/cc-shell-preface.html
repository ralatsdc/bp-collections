PREFACE

“Tangible fragments of life” — “oddball collections of trinkets amidst compendia of data” — “open-ended juxtapositions”. These are all descriptions of the artwork in the form of assemblages by the American artist Joseph Cornell (1903-1972).

In May 2014, Joseph Cornell's assemblage “Untitled (Penny Arcade Portrait of Lauren Bacall)” sold at a Christie's auction for $5.3 million. This sale set an auction record for the self-taught American artist and was far different from anything Joseph Cornell experienced in his lifetime.

Rarely leaving New York City, Joseph Cornell lived in a series of small houses in Queens devoted to caring for his mother and disabled brother, With no formal art training, he assembled everyday found objects - marbles, paper, photos, wood, and glass - into evocative shadowboxes of imaginary worlds. His work is difficult to place within any art movement or style and has inspired generations of American artists.

Unlike Joseph Cornell who assembled physical objects of everyday, Blu Pen uses virtual objects - online content drawn from that vast compendium of everyday human expression, better known as the internet. While Joseph Cornell individually selected and juxtaposed tangible objects to express his personal statement, Blu Pen assembles crowd selected content. Stewart Culin, the late “museum magician” of the Brooklyn Museum, captured Blu Pen's essense well, we are “preserving the seed of things which may blossom and fruit again”.

This collection, Blu Pen's first, orginates from social media content from countries experiencing humanitarian crisis. The content forms a narrative not shaped by a curator, but forged by the internet itself. Insignts and meaning are gained by the viewer as they navigate and explore the collection, a way that performs in the middle ground between print books, ebooks and websites.

We hope you'll enjoy exploring and ultimately be inspired by this Blu Pen collection.
